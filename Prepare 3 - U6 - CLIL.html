The System

The education system in England and Wales is strictly structured into stages: pre-school (3–4), primary (4–11), secondary (11–16), sixth form (16–18), and tertiary (18+).

Over 90% of pupils attend state schools. These are free and follow a national curriculum that standardises subjects, syllabuses, and assessments across the country.

Confusing Fact: Old and prestigious independent schools (like Eton) are called "public schools", even though they are private, fee-paying, and not in the state system.

Types of School

Understanding the different educational environments in the UK system.

Comprehensive School

A state school that accepts pupils of all abilities. It is the most common type of secondary school in the UK, providing education for the vast majority of students without entrance exams.

Grammar School

A selective state school where students must pass an exam (the 11-plus) to get in. These schools focus on academic achievement and are less common than comprehensive schools.

Sixth Form

The final two years of secondary education (ages 16–18). This stage can take place within a secondary school or at a separate "Sixth Form College," and is dedicated to preparing students for A-levels and university.

GCSE

General Certificate of Secondary Education

These are the standard exams taken by almost all students in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland at the age of 16.

Students typically take GCSEs in a range of subjects including Maths, English, and Science. Grades are important for entry into Sixth Form or employment.

Advanced Levels

AS Level

Advanced Subsidiary Level

Exams typically taken after the first year of Sixth Form (Year 12). They are worth 40% of a full A-level (under some specifications) or act as a standalone qualification.

A Level

Advanced Level

The final exams taken at age 18 (Year 13). These are the main qualifications used by universities to assess suitability for higher education courses.
